How People Learn

Carol Dweck, in her lecture, “How to help every child fulfil their potential”, starts with the premise that we all know how to
learn from the day we were born. From that point on, we begin to be sorted into two categories: a growth mindset or a fixed mindset.
A person with a growth mindset believes that intelligence is something that can be developed while a person with a fixed mindset
believes that intelligence is a fixed trait that you have no control over. Dweck sets out the following differences between the
two types of mindsets:

Growth Mindsets

  • Learn at all costs
  • Work hard, effort is key
  • Will learn from mistakes and confront deficiencies

Fixed Mindsets

  • Will try to look smart at all costs
  • Believe you shouldn’t need effort, it should come naturally
  • Will try to hide mistakes and deficiencies

Dweck goes on to say that the growth mindset can be taught. And teaching it causes improvement in learning.

Derek Muller of Veritasium examines preconceived notions and wrong ideas that people have about a variety of facts.
By putting together video snippets of, sometimes hilariously, wrong answers followed by the correct answer, he believes
people are more likely to remember. I call this the “mind blown” technique of teaching. Muller draws a distinction
between unknowledgable and unintelligent. The people he interviews in his Misconception series start out as
unknowledgable on a topic but end up remembering the correct information in the end because of the style of presentation.

Both Dweck and Veritasium believe that people can be taught to process information and learn with greater success.
